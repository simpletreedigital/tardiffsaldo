Elder Law in San Luis Obispo County

When Someone You Love Needs Legal Protection

San Luis Obispo County has one of the highest concentrations of residents over 65 in California. The communities along the Central Coast, from Los Osos to Cambria, from Arroyo Grande to Paso Robles, draw retirees precisely because of the quality of life here. That same population is disproportionately targeted by financial predators, unscrupulous caregivers, and family members who take advantage of diminished capacity.

Elder abuse in California takes many forms. The most common and often the most devastating is financial: forged documents, coerced changes to wills and trusts, unauthorized withdrawals, deed transfers under duress, and outright theft by trusted individuals. Under California Welfare and Institutions Code section 15610.30, elder financial abuse is a specifically defined civil wrong that carries enhanced remedies including attorney's fees, damages, and potential punitive damages. It is also a crime under Penal Code section 368.

We handle elder law matters at Tardiff and Saldo with the urgency they require. Delay in financial abuse cases allows assets to be dissipated, documents to be destroyed, and transfers to be completed. If you suspect an older adult is being exploited or has already been harmed, contact us immediately. The SLO County Adult Protective Services handles emergency referrals, and we coordinate with APS when appropriate.

Elder Law Services We Provide

  • Elder Financial Abuse Claims: When an older adult has been the victim of financial exploitation, we pursue civil claims under the Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act (EADACPA). This statute provides for recovery of attorney's fees, costs, and enhanced damages in qualifying cases, making it possible to pursue claims even when the financial loss alone would not justify the cost of litigation.
  • Trust and Will Contest Arising from Undue Influence: Many elder financial abuse cases involve a trusted person, a caregiver, a new romantic partner, or an estranged family member who gains influence over an elderly person and causes them to change their estate plan. We challenge these transfers in court when they result from undue influence, fraud, or lack of capacity. Our litigation background makes us effective in these technically demanding cases.
  • Conservatorship: When an older adult can no longer manage their own financial or personal affairs and does not have an adequate power of attorney in place, a court-supervised conservatorship may be necessary. We represent petitioning family members in conservatorship proceedings in SLO County Superior Court and advise on the ongoing duties of a court-appointed conservator.
  • Medi-Cal Planning: Long-term care in California is expensive. A private room in a skilled nursing facility in SLO County costs $120,000 to $160,000 per year or more. Medi-Cal can cover these costs for qualifying individuals, but the eligibility rules are complex and the look-back period for asset transfers is five years. We advise families on lawful Medi-Cal planning strategies before and during the application process.
  • Power of Attorney Review and Challenges: A durable power of attorney is the most powerful legal document in everyday use. We review existing powers of attorney for scope and validity, advise families when an agent is abusing their authority, and take legal action to revoke or challenge powers of attorney obtained by fraud or undue influence.
  • Estate Planning for Aging Adults: For older clients who have not yet created an estate plan, or whose existing plan does not address long-term care, capacity, or Medi-Cal considerations, we create comprehensive plans that account for the specific legal challenges that arise as we age. This includes healthcare directives, POLST coordination, and successor trustee planning for cognitive decline scenarios.
Client Case Study

Financial Exploitation by a Caregiver: Recovering What Was Taken

The Situation

An 81-year-old Morro Bay resident with early-stage dementia had been living with a hired caregiver for two years. Family members discovered that the caregiver had obtained a power of attorney, transferred $140,000 from the client's bank accounts, and changed the beneficiary on a life insurance policy naming herself. The client's trust had not been updated to reflect these changes.

Our Approach

We filed an emergency petition in SLO County Superior Court seeking immediate revocation of the power of attorney and a temporary restraining order on further asset transfers. Simultaneously, we filed a civil complaint under the Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act. We coordinated with Adult Protective Services and a forensic accountant to trace all transfers. The caregiver's criminal prosecution ran parallel to our civil case.

The Outcome

The civil case settled for full recovery of the $140,000 plus attorney's fees awarded under EADACPA. The life insurance beneficiary designation was restored. The criminal matter resulted in a plea with restitution ordered. We then updated the client's estate plan with appropriate capacity protections and a professional fiduciary as successor trustee.

Recognizing Elder Financial Abuse: Warning Signs

Financial exploitation of older adults is often difficult to detect because it is carried out by people the elder trusts. Warning signs include: sudden changes to a will, trust, or beneficiary designations, particularly after a new person enters the elder's life; unexplained withdrawals or transfers from bank accounts; unpaid bills when income should cover them; the elder appearing fearful of a caregiver or family member; new names on the elder's bank accounts or deed; the elder being isolated from family and friends; confusion about financial matters the elder previously handled competently.

If you observe any of these signs, do not wait. Contact Adult Protective Services at (805) 781-1775 for emergencies and call our office at (888) 461-2215 for legal guidance. The faster an elder financial abuse claim is initiated, the better the chances of recovering assets before they are spent or hidden.

California Elder Abuse Law: What Makes These Cases Different

Elder financial abuse cases under EADACPA are civil cases with a significantly different structure than standard civil litigation. The statute requires proof that the defendant took, appropriated, or retained property of an elder for wrongful use or with intent to defraud, or through undue influence. When this is established, the prevailing plaintiff recovers attorney's fees and costs as a matter of right, not discretion. In cases involving a fiduciary who acted recklessly or with malice, punitive damages may also be available.

This fee-shifting provision is what makes it practical to pursue elder abuse cases involving smaller amounts of money that would otherwise not justify litigation. We have handled elder abuse cases involving amounts from $30,000 to over $500,000, and the EADACPA structure makes more of these cases economically viable to prosecute. See the California Welfare and Institutions Code section 15610.30 for the statutory definition of financial abuse. The California Department of Social Services APS page provides statewide resources.

Common Questions

Elder Law FAQs

What counts as elder financial abuse in California?+
California Welfare and Institutions Code section 15610.30 defines elder financial abuse broadly. It includes taking, appropriating, or retaining an elder's property for wrongful use, with intent to defraud, or through undue influence. The elder does not need to have dementia or any diagnosed cognitive impairment. Financial abuse can occur against a mentally competent elder who was manipulated, deceived, or pressured. The definition covers thefts, forged documents, coerced transfers, and abuse of a power of attorney, among other conduct.
Can I report elder abuse anonymously in San Luis Obispo County?+
Yes. Reports to SLO County Adult Protective Services can be made anonymously. APS can be reached at (805) 781-1775 for non-emergencies. For emergencies, call 911. Mandated reporters in California, including physicians, nurses, social workers, and financial institution employees, are required by law to report known or suspected elder abuse. Family members and friends are not mandated reporters but are strongly encouraged to report. APS reports trigger an investigation separate from any civil or criminal proceedings.
Is it too late to pursue an elder abuse claim if the elder has passed away?+
No. A civil claim under EADACPA survives the elder's death and can be brought by the personal representative or successor trustee of the estate. In practice, many elder financial abuse claims are discovered during estate administration when the family sees for the first time the full picture of what was taken. The statute of limitations in most cases is four years from the date of discovery of the abuse, though the analysis is fact-specific.
What is a conservatorship and when is it necessary?+
A conservatorship is a court-supervised arrangement in which a judge appoints a person or professional fiduciary to manage an incapacitated adult's finances, personal care, or both. It is necessary when an older adult lacks the capacity to make decisions and does not have a valid, operative power of attorney or trust with successor trustee provisions that cover incapacity. Conservatorship is a last resort because it is expensive, time-consuming, and restrictive of the conservatee's autonomy. Proper estate planning with durable powers of attorney and successor trustee designations can often make conservatorship unnecessary.
How do I protect an aging parent who is resisting estate planning?+
This is one of the most common and difficult elder law questions. A competent adult has the right to decline estate planning, and no one can force them to create documents. The practical approaches include: addressing their specific concerns directly (many elders worry about losing control, which a properly structured plan actually prevents); involving their physician or a geriatric care manager; providing third-party information about what happens without a plan; and ensuring that the attorney they work with meets with them privately to confirm capacity and absence of undue influence. We handle these situations regularly and can advise on realistic approaches for your family's specific circumstances.
